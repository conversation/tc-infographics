Tax+expand

Under Labor, only businesses with a turnover of under A$2 million will receive a tax cut.

As part of its ten-year economic plan, Labor will target family tax benefits, tightening the means test for Family Tax Benefit A and reducing the payment by 50% for families with incomes of more than $100,000.

Labor claims its raise in the tobacco excise by 12.5% a year for four years will raise an extra $19.5 billion compared to the Coalition’s promise.

Labor will also confine negative gearing to new housing and halve the capital gains subsidy for assets bought after July 1, 2017 – from 50% to 25%.

Labor's tax avoidance plan now is to focus more on tightening up the rules that regulate excessive tax-deduction claims, and improving data-matching rules to achieve better compliance.

Jobs+expand

Labor's Working Futures Program offers a six-week “work-readiness” course, a six-month work placement with an employer (paid at a training wage, a percentage of the award wage) and a Certificate III in a subject of their choice on successful completion. Labor has also promised to overcome barriers to employment in disadvantaged communities.

A more contentious jobs plan is Labor's A$100 million bailout for the South Australian steelmaker Arrium.

However, Labor has not tackled the issue of underemployment.

Labor has committed to establish ten Commonwealth Institutes of Higher Education as part of its argument that well-resourced education is crucial to developing skills that will help people find jobs and foster economic growth.

Economic management+expand

Labor hopes its ten-year economic plan will return the budget to surplus in 2020-21 – the same timing as the previous Coalition plan. But it has indicated it would allow the deficit to worsen over the short term.

Labor's savings include tightening the Family Tax Benefit system and tackling so-called "zombie" measures that the Turnbull government was unable to get through the parliament.

The secretaries of Treasury and Finance have warned it is “crucial” for Australia to retain its AAA credit rating, and said surpluses won’t be possible without tax rises unless there is a “considerable effort” to reduce spending growth.

Labor has also claimed savings from rejecting the Coalition's proposed company tax cuts.

Education+expand

During the election Labor shifted the early-years education debate from being about getting mums back to work to delivering high-quality, affordable education. It plans to invest A$3 billion into child care, lift the annual cap on the Child Care Rebate from $7,500 to $10,000 per child, and increase the Child Care Benefit by 15% starting January 1, 2017.

Both parties were committed to giving extra "needs-based" funding to schools. Labor’s promise is $4.5 billion for 2018-19 as part of the Gonski reform model.

For Labor, "needs-based" means allocating funding to support disadvantaged children who need it the most – which includes Indigenous children and those with disability.

Strengthening teacher quality, early intervention programs in every school, increasing Year 12 completion rates to 95% by 2020, and focusing on improving STEM, coding and entrepreneurial skills are also priorities.

Labor's plan is to create ten Institutes of Higher Education, increase funding by $2,500 per student each year from 2018, and impose an $8,000-a-year cap on VET student loans. It also aims to boost science spending by $375 million, restore funding to the CSIRO and university research, and help regional universities to increase their research effort.

Labor will also spend $400 million on scholarships worth $15,000 aimed at getting STEM graduates into teaching. Shadow Higher Education Minister Kim Carr also said he would establish a department of innovation.

Those who take out student loans will soon have to start paying the debt back once they start earning $50,638, down from $54,869. Some nursing and teaching graduates will avoid these reductions.

Health+expand

Labor's "protecting Medicare" campaign centred on lifting the Coalition's rebate freeze and maintaining bulk-billing incentives for pathology (blood and tissue tests) and radiology (x-rays and MRIs). These promises are projected to cost A$2.4 billion and $884 million respectively over the next four years.

Shorten has promised his government will increase hospital funding to the states by $4.9 billion and will fund 50% of the growth in hospital costs.

Labor’s only health savings measures are in private health insurance. It will save $3 billion by freezing the private health insurance rebate for a further five years and removing the rebate for natural therapies. These changes will leave many Australians with higher out-of-pocket costs for their insurance.

Labor will also remove the private health insurance rebate from "junk policies" that only cover public hospital treatment, saving $135 million over the forward estimates.

Labor has promised to spend $300 million over four years on prevention programs to tackle obesity, health literacy, smoking and binge drinking, and will invest in suicide-prevention strategies and programs.

Environment+expand

Labor will aim for a 45% reduction in emissions by 2030 relative to 2005 levels.

Labor has a mandate for a phased-in emissions trading schemes for power generators and other heavy industries, despite the vitriol that was poured on Julia Gillard's carbon tax during the 2013 campaign.

Labor will also aim to generate 50% of Australia's energy through renewables.

Labor has pledged A$500 million over five years to help protect the Great Barrier Reef – including $123 million as a continuation of an existing Coalition pledge – to be split between scientific research, pollution reduction and restoration projects, and reef management.

The funding details are complicated, but one point is clear: none of it comes close to what experts say is needed.

Among the other of Labor's "100 Positive Policies" that relate to the environment are promises to introduce a national interest test for domestic gas exports, and to look at high-speed rail, while also promising a flight curfew over the planned Badgerys Creek airport to help western Sydney get some sleep.

Asylum seekers+expand

Labor is standing by the policy that no asylum seekers who arrive by boat will ever be resettled in Australia. This, Labor claims, will act as a deterrent to the people-smuggling trade from restarting.

Bill Shorten has vowed to send his immigration minister to Geneva on the day he is sworn in to re-engage with the UN High Commissioner for Refugees to find a viable third-country resettlement option.

Infrastructure+expand

Labor strongly supports a number of public transport projects including include the Melbourne Metro, Perth Metronet, Brisbane Cross Rail Link and AdeLINK. It also supports a second Sydney airport at Badgerys Creek served by a rail link.

Before the election, Labor committed to funding WestConnex, the M1 and Ipswich Motorway upgrades in Brisbane, the Gold Coast Light Rail and Metronet in Perth.

Labor is proposing a A$10 billion "concrete bank" – based on the Clean Energy Finance Corporation model – to finance projects according to Infrastructure Australia priorities.

Housing policy challenges Labor now faces include ensuring homes are affordable, equitable and meet changing needs.

In terms of the high-speed broadband network, Labor plans to restore its original fibre-to-the-premises model.

Finally, green infrastructure is coming into its own. Water security and heat-adapted urban environments, as well as more resilient and sustainable infrastructure in general, are prerequisites for Labor to create the best cities we can in a time of climate change.

Science+expand

Years of budget cuts have forced universities, research institutions and the CSIRO to tighten their belts and trim staff. This has sometimes compromised their ability to conduct top-tier research.

Funding has also been excruciatingly tight for individual researchers. Fewer than 15% of grant applications to the National Health and Medical Research Council (NHMRC) are funded each year.

In light of this, Labor promised to “restore university research funding” by restoring block grant funding and reducing university reliance on student fees to fund research.

Labor is also aspiring to lift the proportion of GDP spent on research from the current 2.1% to 3% by 2030.

Specific funding includes A$250 million to the CSIRO; $60.4 million for a new research vessel for the Australian Institute of Marine Science, to be built at an Australian shipyard, naturally; $76.9 million to establish an Australian Institute for Biosecurity; and $500 million to help protect the Great Barrier Reef.

Social policy+expand

As opposition leader, Bill Shorten committed to improving gender equity if he became prime minister.

Labor's key childcare policy pitch has been to bolster payments through the Child Care Rebate and Child Care Benefit as well as open up more childcare places.

Labor pledged strong support to tackle family violence during the election campaign. The party promised an interim package of A$70 million over three years to fund a suite of measures, including five days of paid family violence leave.
